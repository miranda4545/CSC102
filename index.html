Abstract:

Our group paper will focus on modern internet voting. We will look at methods for ensuring safe and secure voting. Issues with internet voting such as preventing voter fraud, certifying voter identity, and cybersecurity issues will be discussed. Especially today with the COVID-19 pandemic, the need for alternative methods of voting has become more prevalent. Countries such as Switzerland and France have established methods for internet or electronic voting and our paper will look at approaches these countries have undertaken for successful implementation of internet voting. Problems and controversies that have occured from internet voting will also be discussed. Lastly, our paper will look at any recent developments and work on internet voting.

Future Advances

A strange trend in voting recently has actually been states trying to remove electronics from the voting system. This is partially through fear of the elections being hacked, but also largely also because the voting machines themselves have encountered many technical problems, creating machines that are both expensive and useless. So, many states have chosen to instead go back to people filling out paper ballots. Moving back to the paper ballot also allows states to do recounts much more reliably than any electronic system. To us, this might mean that online voting, when it does come, may actually look more like paper voting. One way to have more reliable online voting could be to send everyone a mail in ballot, have them fill it out, send a secure scan of the ballot (like when depositing a check online) and finally have people still send in their ballots like standard mail-in voting. This “mail-voting plus” could help with many of the problems associated with mail-in voting in 2020. The online scan could be processed quickly on election day to get a quicker count of the votes, but a recount could still happen with the mailed in paper ballot if necessary. It would also mean that people could still vote on election day and send in their mail ballot that day without worrying about their vote being counted.

Methods

One example of real-world deployment of remote Internet voting that works and continues today is in the state of Geneva, Switzerland. Switzerland is unique because of its direct democracy model in which citizens can vote four to six times every year on referendums. This frequen increased the need for voting accessibility. Internet voting is still under trial, which means that a maximum of 20 percent of Swiss voters can use the online option, while the rest vote by mail. There has been no visible effect on voter turnout; however, one study shows that elimination of voter errors (such as incorrectly marked boxes) and spoiled ballots, known as “residual votes,” has increased the effective turnout of valid and counted votes (Germann, 2020). Geneva manages security concerns in three ways: on the voter’s PC, the Internet, and the state’s IT system. The voter’s PC is the most vulnerable link, so a signed java applet is sent to the PC and stored on the voting server for the entire voting session to protect against malicious software (viruses) on the host PC. The applet creates an additional layer of encryption inside the Transport Layer Security (TLS) and validates votes by comparing each one against all possible answers, rejecting any that do not fit this referential. The data is protected on the Internet by the java applet server, which is encrypted using random numbers. Finally, the state’s IT system is protected by deactivating irrelevant functions, managing interactions between system components, and filtering requests and commands. The state of Geneva claims they have created a strong “fully controlled perimeter” by “extend[ing] the state control as far as possible in order to recreate as much as possible the situation of the polling station.”
